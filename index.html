So what are these tax brackets that everyone talks about?

The US uses graduated tax brackets. This means everyone's income in the US is taxed in chunks. Each chunk has a tax rate. We call these chunks brackets.

Calculating taxes on this system ensures that each citizen is tax uniformly and fairly. That is to say, everyone pays the same percentage in each bracket. As you begin to make more money, you shift into higher brackets where each new dollar is taxed at a higher rate.

You might be delighted to know that Donald Trump pays the same amount of money on his first $9,225 as you do. The difference between you and ole Trumpy boy is that he is paying significantly more money on each dollar that he makes over your highest tax bracket. You do probably have better hair, though. So there's that.

Still in the weeds on this whole tax bracket thing? That's cool. Just keep reading.

MYTH:

"If I work another job it might bump me into a new tax bracket and cause me to loose money."

FACT:

Only the "new" money that you earned at your second job will be taxed at the "new" rate.

Math time!

Ok, so let's take the information from the previous two images and apply some simple math to figure out how much we would owe the government. Notice how these numbers match the numbers on the bottom of our horizontal bar.

10% of $9,225 is $922.50
15% of $28,225 is $4,233.75
25% of $12,550 is $3,137.50

So, you would owe the federal gov $8,293.75 before deductions.
